\begin{abstract}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The Ariel mission will characterize the atmospheres of
          $\sim\!1000$ exoplanets, demanding statistical tools that
          infer robust population-level trends.
    \item We present HERMES (HiERarchical Modelling for Exoplanet
          Science), a multidimensional Bayesian framework that probes
          population-level constraints across planetary mass, planetary
          atmospheric metallicity, and stellar metallicity.
    \item Starting from the Ariel Mission Candidate Sample (MCS,
          618~planets), we inject plausible multidimensional trends and
          demonstrate successful parameter recovery.
    \item Simulated survey samples are generated with controlled
          leverage and sample size, in the presence of measurement
          noise and intrinsic astrophysical scatter.
    \item We fit independent Bayesian models to each survey and assess
          whether survey leverage remains a reliable predictor of trend
          precision under intrinsic variance.
    \item For parameters with significant measurement uncertainties
          (e.g., stellar metallicity), we investigate whether leverage
          alone is sufficient to constrain multidimensional trends.
    \item Via hierarchical modelling, we seek to more precisely
          constrain parameter uncertainties by distinguishing intrinsic
          variance from the underlying population trends.
\end{itemize}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Ariel will deliver transmission and emission spectra for a
          large, statistically meaningful sample of exoplanet
          atmospheres.
    \item A central science question: how does atmospheric metallicity
          ($\log X_{\mathrm{H_2O}}$) scale with planetary mass
          ($\log M$) and host-star metallicity
          ($[\mathrm{Fe/H}]_\star$)?
    \item Solar system giants follow an inverse mass--metallicity
          relation; Ariel aims to test this across a diverse exoplanet
          population.
    \item Target selection strategy matters: the \emph{leverage} of a
          survey (spread in the independent variable) controls how
          precisely slopes can be recovered.
    \item Prior work on Ariel target selection:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Edwards \& Tinetti: tier-based optimization.
        \item Burt \& Zellem: photometric precision requirements.
        \item Cowan \& Coll-Neveu: leverage-based target selection.
        \item D'Aoust et al.; Changeat \& Yip: machine-learning
              approaches.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Prior work on mass--metallicity trends:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Welbanks et al.\ (2019): Bayesian retrievals.
        \item Lustig-Yaeger et al.: transmission spectroscopy
              predictions.
        \item Swain et al.: water abundance scaling.
    \end{itemize}
    \item HERMES extends the 1D mass--metallicity framework to a 3D
          model (mass $+$ stellar metallicity $\to$ planetary
          metallicity) with explicit treatment of measurement errors
          and intrinsic scatter.
\end{itemize}

\section{Data and Survey Design}\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Mission Candidate Sample}\label{sec:mcs}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Synthetic dataset: 618~planets drawn from the Ariel MCS.
    \item Columns used:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\log M$: log planetary mass (Jupiter masses).
        \item $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]_\star$: host-star metallicity with
              asymmetric error bars.
        \item $\log X_{\mathrm{H_2O}}$: planetary atmospheric
              metallicity (response variable) with asymmetric error
              bars.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Ground truth: a plausible mass--metallicity trend
          ($\log X_{\mathrm{H_2O}} = -1.09\,\log M - 0.95
          + \mathcal{N}(0,\,0.53)$) is injected; recovery of this
          trend validates the framework.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Nested Mass Classes}\label{sec:classes}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Surveys are drawn using a nested mass-class scheme
          (Figure~\ref{fig:nested}):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{S1}: full catalog (all masses).
        \item \textbf{S2}: $\log M \geq Q_{25}$ (75\% of planets).
        \item \textbf{S3}: $\log M \geq Q_{50}$ (50\% of planets).
        \item \textbf{S4}: $\log M \geq Q_{75}$ (25\% of planets).
    \end{itemize}
    \item Moving S1 $\to$ S4 progressively removes low-mass planets
          $\Rightarrow$ systematically reduces mass leverage.
    \item For each class and sample size
          $N \in \{20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 75, 80, 100, 150\}$,
          eight surveys are drawn without replacement
          ($N_{\mathrm{reps}} = 8$).
    \item Total: up to $4 \times 9 \times 8 = 288$ surveys.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{Ariel_logM_nested_counts.pdf}
\caption{Nested mass-class scheme S1--S4. Each successive class
removes the lowest-mass quartile, reducing mass leverage while
preserving the high-mass tail. Surveys are drawn from each class
independently.}
\label{fig:nested}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Leverage}\label{sec:leverage}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Two independent leverage axes are tracked per survey:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:leverage}
        L_{\mathrm{mass}} = \sqrt{\sum_i (m_i - \bar{m})^2},
        \qquad
        L_{\mathrm{stellar}} = \sqrt{\sum_i (s_i - \bar{s})^2}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    \item Leverage $= \sqrt{N}\;\sigma_x$: grows with both spread and
          sample size.
    \item $L_{\mathrm{mass}}$ is the natural lever arm for
          $\beta_p$ (mass slope); $L_{\mathrm{stellar}}$ for
          $\beta_s$ (stellar slope).
    \item Figure~\ref{fig:design}: the $N$--$L$ design space shows
          how survey class and sample size jointly determine the
          available leverage.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{hermes_met_design_space_N_Lcontours.pdf}
\caption{Survey design space: sample size $N$ vs.\ mass leverage
$L_{\mathrm{mass}}$. Each point is one survey; colors indicate mass
class (S1--S4). Contours show the density of the design space. S1
surveys span the widest mass range and achieve the highest leverage at
every $N$.}
\label{fig:design}
\end{figure}

\section{The MetModel}\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Science Equation}\label{sec:science_eq}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The MetModel relates planetary atmospheric metallicity $y$
          (log space) to centered planetary mass $m_c$ and centered
          host-star metallicity $s_c$:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:metmodel}
        y_i = \alpha_p + \beta_p\, m_{c,i} + \beta_s\, s_{c,i}
              + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{int}},
    \end{equation}
    %
    where:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\alpha_p$: intercept (population mean metallicity).
        \item $\beta_p$: mass slope (the mass--metallicity relation).
        \item $\beta_s$: stellar metallicity slope.
        \item $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{int}}$: intrinsic astrophysical
              scatter beyond measurement noise.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Centering: $m_c = \log M - \overline{\log M}$ and
          $s_c = s_{\mathrm{true}} - \overline{s_{\mathrm{true}}}$
          (computed per survey, per MCMC draw for the latent stellar
          values).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Priors}\label{sec:priors}

\begin{itemize}
    \item All priors are data-informed (weakly informative, scaled to
          the data range within each survey):
    %
    \begin{align}
        \alpha_p &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\bar{y},\;
                      \sigma_y / \sqrt{N}\bigr),
                      \label{eq:prior_alpha} \\
        \beta_p  &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\;
                      \Delta y / \Delta m\bigr),
                      \label{eq:prior_betap} \\
        \beta_s  &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(1,\;
                      \Delta y / \Delta s\bigr),
                      \label{eq:prior_betas} \\
        \varepsilon &\sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(\sigma_y),
                      \label{eq:prior_eps}
    \end{align}
    %
    where $\Delta y$, $\Delta m$, $\Delta s$ denote the span
    (max $-$ min) of the respective variables.
    \item $\beta_p$ centered at $0$: no \emph{a priori}
          preference for positive or negative mass slope.
    \item $\beta_s$ centered at $1$ (``Model~B''): encodes the
          expectation that planetary and stellar metallicities
          correlate roughly one-to-one.
    \item $\varepsilon$: half-normal ensures positivity;
          scale $\sigma_y$ is weakly informative.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Measurement-Error Treatment}\label{sec:errors}

\paragraph{Stellar metallicity (latent variable model).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Stellar metallicity has non-negligible measurement error
          $\Rightarrow$ treating $s_{\mathrm{obs}}$ as exact would
          bias $\beta_s$ (attenuation bias).
    \item A latent true value is introduced per planet:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:latent_s}
        s_{\mathrm{true},i} \sim \mathcal{N}\bigl(s_{\mathrm{obs},i},
        \;\sigma_{s,\mathrm{meas},i}\bigr),
    \end{equation}
    %
    where $\sigma_{s,\mathrm{meas},i} = \tfrac{1}{2}
    (|e_{\mathrm{lo},i}| + |e_{\mathrm{hi},i}|)$ is the symmetrized
    measurement error.
    \item Centered: $s_{c,i} = s_{\mathrm{true},i}
          - \overline{s_{\mathrm{true}}}$, with the mean over latent
          values recomputed each MCMC draw.
    \item Adds $N$ latent parameters per survey but correctly
          propagates stellar measurement uncertainty into the
          posterior.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Planetary metallicity (quadrature likelihood).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Observation noise combines measurement error and intrinsic
          scatter in quadrature:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:obssigma}
        \sigma_{\mathrm{obs},i}
        = \sqrt{\sigma_{p,\mathrm{meas},i}^{2} + \varepsilon^2}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    \item The likelihood:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:likelihood}
        y_i \sim \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu_i,\;
        \sigma_{\mathrm{obs},i}\bigr),
        \quad
        \mu_i = \alpha_p + \beta_p\, m_{c,i} + \beta_s\, s_{c,i}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    \item As $\varepsilon \to 0$, the likelihood tightens to
          measurement noise alone; as
          $\varepsilon \gg \sigma_{p,\mathrm{meas}}$, intrinsic
          scatter dominates.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Mass.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item No error model: $m_c = m - \bar{m}$ enters as a fixed
          array. Mass uncertainties assumed negligible.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Variants}\label{sec:variants}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Three model variants test the necessity of each component:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{B\_full} (baseline):
              $y = \alpha_p + \beta_p m_c + \beta_s s_c + \varepsilon$.
        \item \textbf{A\_no\_scatter}:
              same but $\varepsilon \equiv 0$.
              Tests whether intrinsic scatter is needed.
        \item \textbf{C\_no\_stellar}:
              $y = \alpha_p + \beta_p m_c + \varepsilon$.
              Tests whether stellar metallicity is needed.
    \end{itemize}
    \item In \texttt{C\_no\_stellar}, any real stellar signal becomes
          unmodelled variance $\Rightarrow$ absorbed into
          $\varepsilon$, inflating its posterior.
\end{itemize}

\section{MCMC Configuration}\label{sec:mcmc}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Sampler: NUTS (NumPyro/JAX).
    \item 800 warmup $+$ 800 draws per chain, 1~chain.
    \item \texttt{target\_accept} $= 0.9$.
    \item Each of 288 surveys fit under 3 model variants.
    \item Repeated across 3 independent MCMC seeds (321, 42, 7).
    \item Total: $288 \times 3 \times 3 = 2{,}592$ MCMC fits.
    \item Posterior summaries: mean, SD, 16th/84th percentile HDI
          for $\{\alpha_p, \beta_p, \beta_s, \varepsilon\}$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Posterior Uncertainty vs.\ Leverage}\label{sec:sigma}

\subsection{Expected Scaling}\label{sec:expected}

\begin{itemize}
    \item For ideal linear regression, the slope uncertainty scales as:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:ideal}
        \sigma_{\beta} \propto \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}}{L},
    \end{equation}
    %
    $\Rightarrow$ power-law exponent $\gamma \approx -1$.
    \item In practice, intrinsic scatter and heteroscedastic errors
          modify this.
    \item We fit $\sigma_\theta = a\, L^{\gamma}$ and report
          $\gamma \pm \delta\gamma$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mass Slope Uncertainty}\label{sec:bpsd}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Figure~\ref{fig:lev_bp}: $\sigma_{\beta_p}$ vs.\ both
          leverages at $N=30$, $50$, and $100$.
    \item Strong power-law decay with $L_{\mathrm{mass}}$
          ($\gamma \approx -0.9$): mass leverage is the dominant
          control on mass-slope precision.
    \item Weaker correlation with $L_{\mathrm{stellar}}$: confirms
          $\sigma_{\beta_p}$ is primarily governed by
          $L_{\mathrm{mass}}$.
    \item The scaling relationship is consistent across sample sizes;
          increasing $N$ lowers the overall uncertainty floor but
          preserves the leverage dependence.
    \item S1 (blue, highest leverage) achieves the tightest
          constraints; S4 (red, lowest) the widest.
\end{itemize}

\section{WAIC Model Comparison}\label{sec:waic}

\begin{itemize}
    \item WAIC $= -2\,\mathrm{ELPD}$ computed for each
          survey--model combination.
    \item Two $\Delta$WAIC statistics:
    %
    \begin{align}
        \Delta\mathrm{WAIC}_{\mathrm{scatter}}
            &= \mathrm{WAIC}_{\texttt{A\_no\_scatter}}
             - \mathrm{WAIC}_{\texttt{B\_full}},
             \label{eq:dwaic_scatter} \\
        \Delta\mathrm{WAIC}_{\mathrm{stellar}}
            &= \mathrm{WAIC}_{\texttt{C\_no\_stellar}}
             - \mathrm{WAIC}_{\texttt{B\_full}}.
             \label{eq:dwaic_stellar}
    \end{align}
    %
    Positive $\Delta$WAIC favors \texttt{B\_full}.
    \item Figures~\ref{fig:waic30}--\ref{fig:waic100}: model
          comparison at $N = 30$, $50$, and $100$.
    \item \textbf{Scatter detection}: \texttt{B\_full} wins for the
          vast majority of surveys at every $N$ --- intrinsic scatter
          is robustly detected. The win rate increases with $N$ and
          leverage.
    \item \textbf{Stellar metallicity detection}: \texttt{B\_full}
          rarely wins over \texttt{C\_no\_stellar} --- the stellar
          term improves the posterior but is not decisive for
          prediction at current noise levels. Even at $N=100$ the
          detection rate remains low.
    \item This is scientifically important: the stellar signal is real
          but weak relative to measurement noise $+$ intrinsic
          scatter. The effective detection SNR is
          $\beta_s^2 \mathrm{Var}(s_c) /
          (\sigma_{p,\mathrm{meas}}^2 + \varepsilon^2)$.
    \item Reducing intrinsic scatter (i.e., quieter astrophysical
          populations) would increase the stellar detection rate.
\end{itemize}

\section{Stellar Metallicity Detection}\label{sec:stellar_det}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Figure~\ref{fig:stellar_hist}: histogram of
          $\Delta\mathrm{WAIC}_{\mathrm{stellar}}$ across all surveys.
          Positive values favor \texttt{B\_full} over
          \texttt{C\_no\_stellar}; negative values favor the simpler
          model without the stellar term.
    \item \textbf{Key result}: the full model frequently \emph{loses}
          the WAIC comparison despite the stellar signal being real in
          the synthetic data ($\beta_s = 1$).
    \item \textbf{Why}: the intrinsic astrophysical scatter
          ($\varepsilon \approx 0.53$\,dex) is the same order of
          magnitude as the stellar metallicity values themselves
          ($[\mathrm{Fe/H}]_\star$ spans roughly $-0.5$ to $+0.5$\,dex
          in the MCS).
    \item The effective signal-to-noise ratio for detecting the stellar
          term is:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:stellar_snr}
        \mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{stellar}}
        = \frac{\beta_s^2\,\mathrm{Var}(s_c)}
               {\sigma_{p,\mathrm{meas}}^2 + \varepsilon^2}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    When $\varepsilon^2 \gg \beta_s^2\,\mathrm{Var}(s_c)$, the
    stellar contribution is buried in scatter and WAIC cannot
    distinguish the two models.
    \item \textbf{Contrast with scatter detection}: intrinsic scatter
          is robustly detected because $\varepsilon \approx 0.53$\,dex
          is large relative to measurement noise
          ($\sigma_{p,\mathrm{meas}} \sim 0.1$--$0.3$\,dex). The
          scatter signal is an order of magnitude above the noise
          floor, whereas the stellar signal is not.
    \item This is a physically expected outcome: host-star metallicity
          variations are modest compared to the planet-to-planet
          diversity in atmospheric composition. The $\beta_s$ posterior
          is still well constrained (Section~\ref{sec:bssd}), but the
          \emph{predictive} improvement from including the stellar term
          is small.
    \item Reducing $\varepsilon$ (e.g., a quieter astrophysical
          population or better forward models) would increase stellar
          detection rates.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{histogram.png}
\caption{Histogram of $\Delta\mathrm{WAIC}_{\mathrm{stellar}}$
($= \mathrm{WAIC}_{\texttt{C\_no\_stellar}} -
\mathrm{WAIC}_{\texttt{B\_full}}$) across all surveys. Positive
values favor the full model; negative values favor the model without
stellar metallicity. The distribution is centered near zero,
confirming that the stellar signal is subdominant to intrinsic scatter
at current noise levels.}
\label{fig:stellar_hist}
\end{figure}

\section{Z-Score Calibration}\label{sec:zscores}

\subsection{Definition}\label{sec:zdef}

\begin{itemize}
    \item For each parameter $\theta \in \{\alpha_p, \beta_p, \beta_s,
          \varepsilon\}$ and survey $k$:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:zscore}
        z_\theta^{(k)}
        = \frac{\hat{\theta}^{(k)} - \theta_{\mathrm{ref}}}
               {\sigma_\theta^{(k)}},
    \end{equation}
    %
    where $\hat{\theta}^{(k)}$ is the posterior mean,
    $\sigma_\theta^{(k)}$ is the posterior SD, and
    $\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}$ is the oracle reference from fitting
    \texttt{B\_full} to the full 618-planet catalog.
    \item Well-calibrated posteriors $\Rightarrow$
          $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$:
          $|z| < 1$ for $\approx 68\%$ of surveys,
          $|z| < 2$ for $\approx 95\%$.
    \item $\mathrm{SD}(z) > 1$: uncertainties underestimated.
          $\mathrm{SD}(z) < 1$: overestimated.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Z-Scores vs.\ Leverage}\label{sec:zlev}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Figure~\ref{fig:zbetap}: $z(\beta_p)$ vs.\ both leverages
          --- the most important calibration check.
    \item A flat band at $\pm 1$ with no trend = desired outcome
          (well-calibrated across the design space).
    \item Widening scatter at low $L$ would indicate that
          uncertainties fail to capture limited information in
          narrow-range surveys.
    \item Figure~\ref{fig:zbetas}: $z(\beta_s)$ vs.\ both leverages.
          The right panel ($L_{\mathrm{stellar}}$) is the natural
          driver. Systematic offsets would indicate prior-induced bias.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{z_beta_p_vs_leverage.pdf}

\section{Hierarchical MetModel}\label{sec:hier}

\subsection{Motivation}\label{sec:hier_motiv}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The independent-fit approach treats each survey in isolation.
    \item A hierarchical extension pools information across surveys via
          shared population-level hyperpriors.
    \item Partial pooling: weak-data surveys borrow strength from the
          population; data-rich surveys remain self-determined.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Definition}\label{sec:hier_def}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Population-level hyperpriors:
    %
    \begin{align}
        \mu_{\alpha_p} &\sim \mathcal{N}
            (\bar{y}_{\mathrm{global}},\; 2),
            \nonumber \\
        \mu_{\beta_p}  &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; 5),
            \nonumber \\
        \mu_{\beta_s}  &\sim \mathcal{N}(1,\; 5),
            \label{eq:hier_hyper} \\
        \tau_{\alpha_p},\; \tau_{\beta_p},\;
            \tau_{\beta_s}
            &\sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(1),
            \nonumber \\
        \sigma_\varepsilon &\sim
            \mathrm{HalfNormal}(1).
            \nonumber
    \end{align}
    %
    \item Per-survey parameters (inside a \texttt{numpyro.plate}):
    %
    \begin{align}
        \alpha_p^{(k)} &\sim \mathcal{N}
            (\mu_{\alpha_p},\; \tau_{\alpha_p}),
            \nonumber \\
        \beta_p^{(k)}  &\sim \mathcal{N}
            (\mu_{\beta_p},\; \tau_{\beta_p}),
            \nonumber \\
        \beta_s^{(k)}  &\sim \mathcal{N}
            (\mu_{\beta_s},\; \tau_{\beta_s}),
            \label{eq:hier_survey} \\
        \varepsilon^{(k)} &\sim
            \mathrm{HalfNormal}(\sigma_\varepsilon).
            \nonumber
    \end{align}
    %
    \item Non-centered parameterization to avoid Neal's funnel.
    \item \texttt{max\_tree\_depth}$=12$ for the more complex geometry.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Shrinkage Analysis}\label{sec:shrinkage}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Shrinkage weight:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:shrinkage}
        w_\theta^{(k)}
        = 1 - \frac{\sigma_{\theta,\mathrm{hier}}^{(k)}}
                   {\sigma_{\theta,\mathrm{indep}}^{(k)}}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    \item $w \approx 0$: strong pooling.
          $w \approx 1$: data dominates.
    \item Shrinkage expected to be largest for surveys with small $N$
          and low leverage.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Leverage as the primary design knob}: mass leverage
          $L_{\mathrm{mass}}$ controls mass-slope precision far more
          than increasing $N$ at fixed mass range.
    \item \textbf{Intrinsic scatter}: $\sigma_\varepsilon$ is weakly
          sensitive to survey geometry; it is a population property,
          robustly detected by WAIC.
    \item \textbf{Stellar metallicity}: provides an important but
          only occasionally decisive second-order refinement
          (Section~\ref{sec:stellar_det}). The intrinsic scatter
          ($\varepsilon \approx 0.53$\,dex) is the same order of
          magnitude as the stellar metallicity values themselves,
          drowning the stellar signal in variance. WAIC favors the
          full model primarily for high-leverage, information-rich
          surveys.
    \item \textbf{Measurement-error model}: the latent-variable
          treatment of stellar metallicity correctly propagates
          uncertainty and avoids attenuation bias.
    \item \textbf{Calibration}: z-score analysis validates that
          posteriors are neither overconfident nor overly conservative.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical pooling}: meaningful shrinkage for
          low-leverage surveys demonstrates the potential of
          hierarchical modelling.
    \item Related work: Keating \& Cowan (2022).
\end{itemize}
